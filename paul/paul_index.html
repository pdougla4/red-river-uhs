Overview Why this well

The Williston Basin is strewn with depleted carbonate wellbores approaching economic limit — infrastructure that plug-and-abandon would retire. Stecker 23-3 is one of them. Whiting Oil and Gas spudded it in April 2012, Foundation Energy Management operated it through decline, it produced about 80,000 barrels of oil, and it was plugged and abandoned in August 2025. This project tests a framework for giving wells like Stecker a second productive life: seasonal hydrogen huff-n-puff storage during the energy transition, followed by CO₂-enhanced oil recovery that benefits from residual H₂ exposure of the reservoir rock.

The Red River Formation itself is a convenient proof case — a generic tight dolomitic carbonate with an anhydrite seal — not because it is exceptional, but because it isn't. The geologic criteria that make it viable (tight carbonate matrix, evaporite seal, post-depletion wellbores, documented pore/perm) also describe the Duperow, Nisku, Winnipegosis, and Stony Mountain in this basin, plus the San Andres, Madison, Hunton, and many other carbonate-evaporite systems across the mature US petroleum provinces.

Four-case factorial design

A baseline CO₂-EOR simulation plus three treatment cases in which the reservoir first undergoes a fifteen-year H₂ storage operation at different injection rates. Rock property enhancement from H₂ exposure is parameterized from Aluah et al. (2025), documenting φ increases of +9.7 to +23.1% and k increases of +12.7 to +25.8% depending on saturation history.

Phase 1 H₂ Storage — 15 yr

Three hydrogen huff-n-puff injection rates in a depleted Red River reservoir. Six-month injection cycles alternate with six-month withdrawal cycles for fifteen years. Watch the plume breathe — expanding during injection, retracting during withdrawal. The residual H₂ that persists between cycles is what alters the rock and enables Phase 2's enhancement.

Phase 1 — Low0.5 MMCF/day

Low-rate H₂ huff-n-puff

The plume stays tight around the wellbore. Limited lateral H₂ contact with reservoir rock, which sets an expected lower bound on Phase 2 enhancement.

Phase 1 — Medium2.5 MMCF/day

Medium-rate H₂ huff-n-puff

Cyclic expansion and retraction are clearly visible at this rate. Moderate reservoir contact during each injection half-cycle.

Phase 1 — High5.0 MMCF/day

High-rate H₂ huff-n-puff

Maximum lateral plume extent and longest rock contact time. Greatest expected Phase 2 enhancement — the high end of the dose-response curve.

Phase 2 CO₂-EOR — 25 yr

Four CO₂-EOR simulations through the post-H₂ reservoir. A central CO₂ injector drives a flood toward two offset producers east and west. The only difference between cases is the H₂-exposure history of the rock. Monthly snapshots across twenty-five years of flooding — look for differences in plume penetration, breakthrough timing, and sweep efficiency.

Core Samples Laird Core Library

The reservoir simulation isn't built on theoretical rock properties — it's built on measured data from physical core samples archived at the Wilson M. Laird Core and Sample Library. Cores were sampled from three Red River wells spanning nearly 200 miles across the basin (196.6 mi from #6798 to #14070): Stecker 23-3 itself (Golden Valley County), well #6798 up in Divide County on the northern margin, and well #14070 down in Bowman County near the Cedar Creek Anticline. Samples were selected specifically from the Red River A and B zones — the two target intervals for the dual-phase storage framework, chosen for their combination of porosity, permeability, and effective anhydrite sealing.
